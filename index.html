The air smells faintly of pine and smoke, reminders that nature is constantly changing.

As an Eco-Responder, you've learned that every fire tells two stories: one of loss and one of renewal.

This week, your team is on alert. Just over the ridge, a town called Forest Glen was hit by a wildfire two weeks ago.

Your town, Maple Valley, sits just downwind.

What to Expect

Your job is to find out how wildfires start, spread, and change habitats. You'll look at real evidence from NOAA and FEMA.

Mission Glossary: Key Terms to Know

Fuel-Moisture: How much water is stored in plants. Low moisture means they burn easily.

Defensible Space: A safety zone (30ft) kept clear of flammable materials.

Relative humidity is 14%, winds steady at 25 mph. "We need evidence from Forest Glen before the next spark."

Where will you begin?

After the Fire: Regrouping

You regroup with Jordan and Ranger Marisol. Two reports appear on your tablet: FEMA (human impacts) and Forest Service (ecosystem recovery).

Which evidence will you prioritize?

Areas with defensible space survived. 22 of 30 homes with metal roofs remain.

The manzanita is back, but bare slopes are turning to mud. Nitrate levels in the creek doubled.

"Everything's connected," Jordan says. Neighborhoods with metal roofs had the lowest erosion nearby.

Team Briefing

Team A: Human Impact Report

Defensible space = 70% survival rate. Metal roofs reflected heat better.

Team B: Ecosystem Report

Fire-adapted plants sprouted. Over-cleared areas eroded.

"Same fire, same wind. Different results." Marisol reminds you that vegetation protects soil.

Maple Valley Town Hall

Mayor Elena shares three recovery plans. Which do you recommend?

Plan A: Rebuild Fast

Restore homes quickly. Speed recovery but increases erosion risk.

Plan B: Rebuild Smarter

Fire-resistant materials and native plants. Reduces risk.

Plan C: Balanced Plan

Stages for safety and habitat. Supports both people and nature.
